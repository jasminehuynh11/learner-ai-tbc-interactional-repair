Week 4
Task 1: Discussing Study or Career Plans with an Advisor

English Conversational Partner dijo: Hi, welcome. Take a seat. So, what do you want to focus on today, more study planning, career planning, or like… both?
Dijiste: Hi, thank you. Yeah, both. Because I feel I’m doing okay, but I’m not totally sure it’s the correct direction, and I don’t want to wake up in one year and be like, “why did I do this?”
English Conversational Partner dijo: Yeah, totally fair. Let’s do both. We’ll start with where you are now, then connect it to career pathways. What are you studying, and what year are you in?
Dijiste: Marketing and Communication. And I’m… second year now, yes. Sometimes I feel confident, and sometimes I feel like I’m missing something important.
English Conversational Partner dijo: Okay, second year. When you say “missing something important,” do you mean skills, experience, or like clarity on what job you want?
Dijiste: Honestly… all. I mean, I can do assignments, but in a real job I don’t know what they want from me. And also I worry about my English, like professional English, because I can speak, but sometimes I speak too… casual?
English Conversational Partner dijo: That’s really thoughtful. Professional English is built through practice, not something you just magically have. Let’s step back. After graduation, what kind of work do you imagine, even roughly?
Dijiste: Something with people. Like communication, but not only talking. Like planning, organising, maybe events, or social media. But I don’t want to feel fake, you know? I want to feel… authentic.
English Conversational Partner dijo: Got it. People-focused, planning/organising, maybe social media/events, and authenticity matters to you. Have you had work experience that connects to that?
Dijiste: I work part-time in a café. Not marketing, but I talk to people all the time. And also I do Instagram for my friend’s small business sometimes, like posting. But it’s informal, not official.
English Conversational Partner dijo: That still counts, honestly. Informal work is still real skills. With the Instagram posts, what did you enjoy, and what felt hard?
Dijiste: I enjoy the creative part, like thinking of ideas. The hard part is writing captions because I’m like, “does this sound natural?” I rewrite many times, and then I feel tired.
English Conversational Partner dijo: Makes sense. Writing is often harder than speaking, especially multilingual speakers. Do you feel more confident speaking in meetings, or writing emails?
Dijiste: Speaking, yes. But speaking fast is difficult. Sometimes I start and then I change my sentence in the middle, like… I mean, I restart, because I want to sound more correct.
English Conversational Partner dijo: That’s a classic self-editing habit. It shows you’re monitoring your language, not that you’re weak. We can help you manage it so it doesn’t create stress. On the study side, are you happy with your subjects, or thinking of changing something?
Dijiste: I’m thinking maybe add a minor, or change electives, because some subjects feel too theoretical. Like I learn concepts, but I’m like, “how do I use this in a job?”
English Conversational Partner dijo: Super common feeling. What feels most practical, and what feels least practical?
Dijiste: Practical is group projects, presentations, analysing a campaign. Less practical is when we read many articles and it’s like… abstract. I understand, but I don’t see the bridge to job.
English Conversational Partner dijo: Yeah, you want more applied learning. One option is electives with internships, industry projects, workplace-based learning. Would you be interested in that?
Dijiste: Yes, very interested. But I don’t know how to apply. And I worry my résumé is not strong because I’m international, and I don’t have Australian experience except café.
English Conversational Partner dijo: That’s a real concern, but it’s workable. Café work is Australian experience, and the social media work is relevant too. When you say “résumé not strong,” is it the experience itself, or not knowing how to describe it professionally?
Dijiste: Both. I have experiences, but I don’t know how to make it sound professional without lying.
English Conversational Partner dijo: I like that you care about honesty. You don’t need to lie, you just need clear framing. Like “managed social media content for a small business” can be true even if informal. Another important question: are you worried about visa rules if you change study plan or load?
Dijiste: Yes, exactly. If I take less subjects, or change something, is it dangerous? I don’t want trouble.
English Conversational Partner dijo: You’re right to check. Small changes inside the degree are often okay, but reduced load or course changes can have conditions. Safest step is talking to the international student office first. I can refer you.
Dijiste: Yes please. I prefer to be safe. Another question, sorry I ask too many. If I want marketing job, should I focus on analytics too? Everyone talks about data now, but I’m not very… numeric.
English Conversational Partner dijo: Smart question. You don’t need to be a mathematician, but basic comfort with metrics helps. If you don’t love heavy numbers, you can learn practical analytics at a light level. Do you prefer creative roles, coordination roles, or strategy roles?
Dijiste: Creative and coordination. Strategy sounds scary. I mean, maybe later, but now I want something I can do well.
English Conversational Partner dijo: Great. Content, community, events, marketing coordination could fit. Let’s make it concrete. What’s one action you’d like to take in the next month?
Dijiste: Find an internship, or at least attend a career event. But I don’t know where to start, and I feel nervous to network because I don’t want to sound silly.
English Conversational Partner dijo: That’s very common. We’ll make it lower pressure. I can send events, we can book a résumé review. And we can practise a simple introduction script. Would that help?
Dijiste: Yes, that sounds amazing. If I have a script, I feel calmer.
English Conversational Partner dijo: Perfect. I’ll email events, international office contact, and the résumé appointment link. Anything else you want to clarify today?
Dijiste: Just one more. If I choose an internship subject, will it add more workload? Because I work part-time and I don’t want to burn out.
English Conversational Partner dijo: Great question. Internship subjects replace another subject, so it’s different workload rather than extra. We can check hours and choose something realistic.
Dijiste: Okay, good. Now I feel I have a plan, not only worries.
English Conversational Partner dijo: I’m glad. Quick recap check: people-focused authentic pathway, marketing coordination/content, more applied learning, support with internships, résumé framing, networking, and visa-safe planning. Accurate?
Dijiste: Yes, exactly. Thank you so much.




Task 3: Responding to Feedback or a Performance Review 

English Conversational Partner dijo: Hey, come in. You said you wanted help responding to feedback from work. What happened?
Dijiste: Hi, yes, thank you. I got feedback from my supervisor. It’s not horrible, but I feel… eh… nervous, because I don’t know how to respond correctly without sounding defensive.
English Conversational Partner dijo: That’s a very mature concern. Feedback can feel personal even when it’s practical. Was it written, or said in a meeting?
Dijiste: It was a meeting, but after they sent a short email with the points, so now it feels more official.
English Conversational Partner dijo: Okay, spoken and written. What were the main points? Let’s start with one positive thing.
Dijiste: They said I’m friendly, customers like me, and I’m reliable. So I feel good about that. But then they said I need to be more “assertive” when it’s busy, and I don’t know what they want, because I don’t want to be rude.
English Conversational Partner dijo: Important nuance. Assertive doesn’t mean rude. Usually it means clear, confident, direct when needed. Did they give examples?
Dijiste: They said when customers are waiting, I spend too long explaining, then the line becomes big. But I’m like, I’m trying to be helpful… so I felt confused.
English Conversational Partner dijo: That makes sense. Your intention is helpfulness, but the impact is slower service during peak time. Did you ask them what “assertive” looks like?
Dijiste: No… in the moment my brain went blank. I was smiling, but inside I was like “oh no.”
English Conversational Partner dijo: That happens a lot. You can still ask now, like a follow-up. Would you feel comfortable asking for one or two concrete examples?
Dijiste: Yes, but I don’t know how to say it without sounding like I’m challenging them.
English Conversational Partner dijo: We can keep it collaborative. Like: “Thank you for the feedback, I want to improve. Could you share an example of what assertive communication looks like in busy periods?”
Dijiste: That sounds good. Also I want to explain I’m not slow because I don’t care, it’s because I’m trying to be polite. But maybe I should not explain too much?
English Conversational Partner dijo: Great self-awareness. You can briefly acknowledge intention, but focus on action: “I understand the need to keep service moving. I’ll practise being concise in busy times.”
Dijiste: Concise, yes, that’s the word. Sometimes I talk too much because I want to sound friendly, and also because I’m thinking in my head and speaking at the same time, you know?
English Conversational Partner dijo: Very normal for multilingual speakers. Did they mention anything else?
Dijiste: They also said “take more initiative.” And again I’m like, what initiative? In café job everything is routine. I don’t want to do something and it’s wrong.
English Conversational Partner dijo: Good question. Initiative can be small proactive things: restocking, wiping tables, checking who needs help, without being asked. Does that match your workplace?
Dijiste: Yes, it matches. I do some, but maybe not enough, or they didn’t notice. And I don’t like interrupting people.
English Conversational Partner dijo: Understandable. You can mention you’ll focus on visible proactive actions, especially between busy and quiet moments.
Dijiste: Okay. And should I reply to the email quickly? If I wait, it looks bad. If I reply too fast, my English is messy.
English Conversational Partner dijo: Reply within one or two days is ideal. Keep it simple. Draft it, leave it, reread.
Dijiste: Yes, I like that. Another thing, they said “confidence.” They said I should sound more confident with customers. But I’m confident inside, it’s just my accent. I mean, I have accent and then I feel less confident.
English Conversational Partner dijo: Important point. Accent is not lack of competence. Confidence can show through pace, clarity, short phrases. Want to practise a few set phrases for busy times?
Dijiste: Yes, please. Like short phrases I can repeat without thinking too much.
English Conversational Partner dijo: Perfect. Examples: “I’ll be right with you,” “Thanks for waiting,” “Let me confirm that,” “I can explain more in a moment.”
Dijiste: Oh, I like “I can explain more in a moment.” It’s polite but it keeps the line moving.
English Conversational Partner dijo: Exactly. Do you want to respond by email, or in person?
Dijiste: Email is safer because I can think. In person I become nervous and I say too many things.
English Conversational Partner dijo: Great strategy. In the email: thank them, acknowledge key points, state actions, ask for examples of “assertive” and “initiative.”
Dijiste: Yes. And can I ask for a check-in in some weeks, to see if I improve? Or is that too much?
English Conversational Partner dijo: That’s actually excellent. It shows commitment.
Dijiste: Okay. I feel much better now. I thought feedback means I’m failing, but now I see it’s like… adjustment.
English Conversational Partner dijo: Exactly. Quick check: positive feedback for friendliness/reliability, improvement points were assertiveness in busy times, initiative, confidence. You’ll reply with thanks, clarification request, and concrete actions, and ask for a follow-up check-in. Correct?
Dijiste: Yes, correct. Thank you. I feel calmer, and like I have a plan instead of just feelings.

Week 4
Task 1: Discussing Study or Career Plans with an Advisor
My Words: Hi. I have an appointment about my study plan.
English Conversational Partner said: Yes, welcome. Please take a seat. I see your booking here. Before we start, can you tell me what you’d like to get out of today’s meeting?
My Words: Study plan, but also… future. Like job. I’m confused.
English Conversational Partner said: That’s completely fine, and very common. We can definitely talk about both. Let’s start with your studies, then connect that to career options. What program are you currently enrolled in?
My Words: Business. First year.
English Conversational Partner said: Alright, first-year Business. And how are you finding it so far? Is it matching what you expected?
My Words: Some parts yes. Some parts no. I thought more… practical.
English Conversational Partner said: That makes sense. First year can feel theoretical before things become more applied. Which subjects feel the least practical to you right now?
My Words: Accounting. Also economics. Too many graphs.
English Conversational Partner said: I hear that a lot. When you say “too many graphs”, is it the maths side, or understanding what the graphs mean?
My Words: Meaning. Like… why this graph matters.
English Conversational Partner said: That’s a very important question. Often the “why” isn’t explained well at the beginning. Now, thinking ahead, do you have any idea what kind of work you might want after graduation, even loosely?
My Words: Not really. Office job maybe. But not boring office.
English Conversational Partner said: That’s fair. Most people don’t want boring. When you say “not boring”, do you mean more people-focused, creative, or something else?
My Words: People. Talking. But also stable income.
English Conversational Partner said: That combination actually fits several roles, like marketing coordination, client support, project roles, or account management. Have you had any work experience so far?
My Words: Café job. Customer service.
English Conversational Partner said: That’s very relevant, even if it doesn’t feel academic. Now, can I ask: are you enjoying the café work, or is it just for money?
My Words: Both. I like talking to customers. But pay is low.
English Conversational Partner said: That’s honest, and useful information. It suggests you enjoy interaction, which is important for many business roles. Now, about your study plan, are you enrolled full-time this semester?
My Words: Yes. Four subjects. But I’m tired.
English Conversational Partner said: Four subjects can definitely feel heavy, especially in first year. Are you considering reducing your load, or just feeling overwhelmed right now?
My Words: I was thinking… maybe three subjects. But will that delay graduation?
English Conversational Partner said: Good question. Yes, reducing your load can extend your degree slightly, but many students choose that for wellbeing reasons. It’s not a failure. We’d just need to check visa implications before making changes.
My Words: Visa is important. If I change subject or load, is that risky?
English Conversational Partner said: Minor adjustments within your degree are usually okay, but anything significant should be checked first. I recommend speaking with the international student office before making a decision.
My Words: Okay. Another question… if I don’t like accounting, can I avoid it later?
English Conversational Partner said: You may still need some core subjects, but later you’ll have electives that align more with your interests. We can plan that strategically.
My Words: That makes me feel better.
English Conversational Partner said: Good. Before we finish, is there something you’re worried about that we haven’t talked about yet?
My Words: Grades. If my grades are not high, can I still get job?
English Conversational Partner said: That’s a very common worry. Grades matter, but employers also value skills, experience, and communication. Your café work already gives you an advantage there.
My Words: Okay. So not only grades.
English Conversational Partner said: Exactly. Let me summarise briefly. You’re in first-year Business, finding some subjects too theoretical, interested in people-focused but stable work, and considering workload adjustments. For now, we’ll keep your current enrolment, and I’ll email you resources on academic support, career exploration, and visa advice. Does that sound right?
My Words: Yes. That sounds right.
English Conversational Partner said: Great. You’re asking the right questions at the right time.
My Words: Thank you.

Week 4
Task 3: Responding to Feedback or a Performance Review
My Words: Hi. I got feedback from my supervisor, and I’m not sure what to do.
English Conversational Partner said: That’s okay. Feedback can feel uncomfortable. Let’s go through it together. Was the feedback given in a meeting or in writing?
My Words: Email. Long email.
English Conversational Partner said: Alright. When you read it, what feeling did you have first?
My Words: Stress. Because I don’t know if it’s bad.
English Conversational Partner said: That’s understandable. Was the feedback mostly positive, mostly critical, or mixed?
My Words: Mixed. Some praise, some comments.
English Conversational Partner said: Let’s separate those. What’s one positive thing they mentioned?
My Words: They said I’m reliable and careful.
English Conversational Partner said: That’s strong feedback. Reliability is very valued. Now, what’s one comment that confused you?
My Words: They said I need to be more proactive. I don’t know how much is enough.
English Conversational Partner said: That’s a very good question. In your role, do you usually wait for instructions before acting?
My Words: Yes. Because if I do wrong, I’m scared.
English Conversational Partner said: That makes sense. “Being proactive” usually doesn’t mean taking big risks. It can mean small things, like asking questions earlier or offering help. Does that sound more manageable?
My Words: Yes, but… if I ask too many questions, is that annoying?
English Conversational Partner said: Another excellent question. Usually, asking thoughtful questions is seen as engagement, not annoyance. It depends on timing and tone.
My Words: Okay. Another thing… should I reply to the email quickly, or wait?
English Conversational Partner said: It’s generally good to reply within a day or two. You don’t need a long reply, just a professional one.
My Words: What if my English is not perfect in reply?
English Conversational Partner said: That’s okay. Clear and polite is more important than perfect grammar. You can keep it simple.
My Words: Can I say I will try, even if I’m not sure how?
English Conversational Partner said: Yes, and you can also ask for clarification. For example, you can say you’d like examples of being proactive in your role.
My Words: That feels safer.
English Conversational Partner said: Good. Did the feedback mention any future review or follow-up?
My Words: Yes. Review again in three months.
English Conversational Partner said: That’s helpful. It gives you time to apply the feedback. You might even keep small notes about what you try, so you can show progress later.
My Words: Notes is good idea. Otherwise I forget.
English Conversational Partner said: Exactly. Before we finish, is there anything about the feedback that still feels uncomfortable?
My Words: They didn’t say I’m bad. But I still feel like I did something wrong.
English Conversational Partner said: That feeling is very common. Mixed feedback often feels heavier than it is. Try to see it as guidance, not judgement.
My Words: Okay. I’ll try to think like that.
English Conversational Partner said: Let me summarise. You received mixed feedback, including praise for reliability and a suggestion to be more proactive. You’ll reply professionally, acknowledge the feedback, ask for clarification if needed, and track small improvements. Does that sound right?
My Words: Yes. That sounds right.
English Conversational Partner said: Excellent. You’re handling this thoughtfully.
My Words: Thank you.

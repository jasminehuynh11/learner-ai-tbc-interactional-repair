Week 4 – Task 2: Participating in Group Decisions and Negotiating Responsibilities
You said:
Hi everyone.
Before we start anything, I think we really need to talk about how we are going to work together, because last time in another subject it became very stressful at the end.
English Conversational Partner said:
I think that’s a good idea. Group work can get messy if roles aren’t clear. What part do you want to start with?
You said:
Maybe roles first.
Because if roles are unclear, then people either do nothing or do too much.
English Conversational Partner said:
That’s true. What kind of role do you think suits you best?
You said:
I think I can do research.
I’m okay with reading articles, finding sources, and summarising information.
English Conversational Partner said:
That’s great. How do you feel about presenting or speaking in front of the class?
You said:
Honestly, I feel nervous about that.
I can speak, but when I’m nervous, my English becomes worse.
English Conversational Partner said:
That’s understandable. Would you rather focus on writing instead of presenting?
You said:
Yes, writing is more comfortable.
But… I don’t want to avoid presenting completely, because I know it’s important.
English Conversational Partner said:
So maybe a smaller speaking role?
You said:
Yes, maybe.
Like explaining one slide, not the whole presentation.
English Conversational Partner said:
That sounds reasonable. We can plan it that way.
You said:
Okay.
But writing also has many parts, so I want to be clear which writing part I will do.
English Conversational Partner said:
Good point. We have introduction, literature review, analysis, and conclusion. Which part would you prefer?
You said:
Literature review.
But I want to check if I understand correctly.
English Conversational Partner said:
Sure, go ahead.
You said:
Literature review means summarising what other researchers said, not my opinion, right?
English Conversational Partner said:
Mostly yes. You summarise and compare previous studies, and maybe connect them to our topic.
You said:
Okay, so some connection is allowed.
I thought it’s only summary.
English Conversational Partner said:
You’re right to check. It’s not only summary.
You said:
Alright.
I think I can do that.
English Conversational Partner said:
How about deadlines? Do you think we should set internal deadlines?
You said:
Yes, definitely.
If we don’t, everything will be last minute.
English Conversational Partner said:
What would you suggest?
You said:
Maybe one week before the actual submission.
But I’m not sure if that’s realistic for everyone.
English Conversational Partner said:
That’s a fair concern. Why do you think it might be difficult?
You said:
Because some people work part-time, and sometimes they reply late.
I don’t want to sound controlling.
English Conversational Partner said:
That’s understandable. How could we phrase it so it feels supportive, not controlling?
You said:
Maybe we can say “draft deadline”, not “final deadline”.
English Conversational Partner said:
That’s a good strategy.
You said:
Also, I think regular check-ins help.
Not long meetings, just short messages.
English Conversational Partner said:
How often do you think is reasonable?
You said:
Maybe every three days.
But not too strict.
English Conversational Partner said:
So something flexible but consistent.
You said:
Yes.
Because English discussion meetings are tiring for me, honestly.
English Conversational Partner said:
That’s completely fair. We can keep communication simple.
You said:
Okay, so let me summarise to check if I understood correctly.
My role is research and literature review writing,
small speaking part in presentation,
draft deadline one week early,
and short check-ins every few days.
English Conversational Partner said:
Yes, that matches what we discussed.
You said:
Okay.
I feel much less anxious now.
English Conversational Partner said:
I’m glad. Clear planning really helps.




Week 4 – Task 3: Responding to Feedback or a Performance Review
You said:
Hi, thank you for meeting with me.
I wanted to talk about the feedback on my presentation, because I’ve been thinking about it a lot.
English Conversational Partner said:
Of course. Overall, your presentation had strong content and clear structure.
You said:
Thank you.
I was worried because while speaking, I felt like my mind went blank sometimes.
English Conversational Partner said:
That happens to many people. One thing we noticed was your pacing.
You said:
Pacing…
Do you mean I was too fast or too slow?
English Conversational Partner said:
Mostly too fast in the middle section.
You said:
That’s interesting, because I thought I was being slow.
English Conversational Partner said:
In the beginning you were, but then you sped up.
You said:
Ah, I see.
I think I was watching the time and panicking.
English Conversational Partner said:
That makes sense. Time pressure often affects pacing.
You said:
When you say “slow down”, what should I actually do?
Because telling myself “slow down” doesn’t work.
English Conversational Partner said:
That’s a good question. One concrete thing is to pause between ideas.
You said:
Pause meaning silence?
English Conversational Partner said:
Yes, short silence.
You said:
I always feel silence is awkward, like people will judge me.
English Conversational Partner said:
Many people feel that way, but listeners usually appreciate pauses.
You said:
I never thought about listener’s perspective.
I only think about myself when presenting.
English Conversational Partner said:
That’s very common, especially in a second language.
You said:
What about eye contact?
You mentioned it briefly in the written feedback.
English Conversational Partner said:
Yes. You relied on your slides a bit too much.
You said:
I thought looking at slides is respectful.
But maybe it looks like I’m avoiding audience?
English Conversational Partner said:
It can give that impression, yes.
You said:
Okay.
So I should look at audience even if I feel uncomfortable?
English Conversational Partner said:
You don’t need constant eye contact, just brief moments.
You said:
Alright.
Another thing I worry about is pronunciation.
English Conversational Partner said:
Your pronunciation was mostly clear.
You said:
Mostly?
So sometimes not clear?
English Conversational Partner said:
There were a few words, but context helped.
You said:
Context meaning the sentence helped people guess?
English Conversational Partner said:
Exactly.
You said:
Okay, that makes me feel better.
I was worried people didn’t understand anything.
English Conversational Partner said:
That’s not the case at all.
You said:
If I want to improve pronunciation, what should I focus on first?
English Conversational Partner said:
Stress and intonation rather than individual sounds.
You said:
Stress meaning which word is stronger?
English Conversational Partner said:
Yes.
You said:
Okay.
That feels more manageable.
English Conversational Partner said:
Would you like to set a goal for your next presentation?
You said:
Yes.
My goal is to pause more, speak slower, and not panic about time.
English Conversational Partner said:
That’s a very realistic goal.
You said:
Thank you.
Talking about this in detail really helps.
English Conversational Partner said:
I’m glad. You’re making steady progress.

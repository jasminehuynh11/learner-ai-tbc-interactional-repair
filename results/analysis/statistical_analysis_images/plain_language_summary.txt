PLAIN-LANGUAGE SUMMARY 
================================================================================

HOW OFTEN DO REPAIRS HAPPEN?

Repairs are relatively infrequent in learner-AI dialogues. On average, each dialogue 
contains 1.26 repair sequences (median: 1). Approximately 
48.8% of dialogues contain no repairs, 16.3% contain exactly one repair, 
and 34.8% contain two or more repairs. When repairs do occur, they usually 
involve 1-2 sequences; only a small handful of dialogues show very high repair activity 
(up to 8 repairs).


WHO INITIATES REPAIRS AND ARE THEY RESOLVED?

Most repairs are learner-initiated (LI, n = 428, 73.0%) rather than 
bot-initiated (BI, n = 155, 26.5%). Across both types, the vast majority 
of repairs are resolved (86.0% resolution rate), with only a small number 
of unresolved cases. The chi-square test shows no strong statistical evidence that 
learner- vs bot-initiated repairs differ in resolution success in this pilot sample 
(χ² = 404.3696, p = 0.0000).


WHAT CAUSES REPAIRS?

The most common triggers involve vocabulary/lexical issues (31.1%), 
followed by vocabulary/lexical problems, ASR/pronunciation issues, and self-correction. 
Bot misunderstandings and contextual errors are relatively rare but important, as they 
highlight system design limitations. These patterns align with theoretical expectations 
that vocabulary and comprehension problems are typical sites of negotiation for meaning 
in second language learning contexts.


HOW DO REPAIRS RELATE TO PARTICIPATION?

Dialogues with more repairs also tend to have more turns (r = 0.352, 
p = 0.000), which may reflect more engaged, extended interactions rather than purely 
'problematic' sessions. We did not find strong evidence that unresolved repairs are 
systematically associated with longer or more verbose sessions—unresolved repairs are 
rare in this dataset (80 
cases out of 586 total repairs).


IMPLICATIONS FOR SYSTEM & PEDAGOGY

• Support vocabulary scaffolding: Given that vocabulary and comprehension issues are 
  common triggers, the system should provide contextual definitions and examples.

• Improve ASR robustness and clarification strategies: ASR/pronunciation issues 
  account for a notable proportion of repairs; better speech recognition and 
  clarification protocols could reduce these.

• Use repair patterns as indicators of productive struggle rather than failure: 
  The positive correlation between repairs and turns suggests that repairs occur in 
  more engaged dialogues, indicating active learning rather than breakdown.

• Design for learner-initiated repairs: Since most repairs are learner-initiated, 
  the system should support learners' self-monitoring and clarification requests.

• Monitor resolution success: The high resolution rate (86.0%) 
  suggests the current system effectively supports repair sequences, but attention 
  should be paid to the small number of unresolved cases.
